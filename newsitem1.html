Unmasking Marshmello: The Real Identity Of The $21 Million DJ
Natalie Robehmed Forbes Staff
Media & Entertainment

Marshmello, real name Chris Comstock, is a masked DJ-producer known for hits including "Alone" and "Silence."

In the middle of a 2015 on-air interview with Yahoo!'s Katie Couric, Skrillex's phone rang. "It's Chris," Couric noted.

"Oh, Marshmello," Skrillex responded, before picking up the phone and putting DJ-producer Marshmello on speaker.

The interaction ignited a debate around the masked newcomer's identity, who had begun releasing music online for free just three months earlier. Social media detectives and EDM fans have since believed Marshmello to be an alias of Chris Comstock, also known as Dotcom, a DJ-producer on the same management roster as Marshmello. Forbes can now conclusively report that they are the same person.

Skrillex in a 2015 interview with Couric, holding up his phone with Comstock's name saved as "Chris Marshmello" (Image courtesy Yahoo!)Image courtesy Yahoo!

Masked performers have been omnipresent in electronic music, from mouse head-wearing Deadmau5 to the robot-helmeted Daft Punk. But Marshmello's stratospheric rise has been faster than that of any of his hidden compatriots: Ingenious marketing and infectious hits propelled him to Forbes' annual ranking of the world's highest-paid DJs less than two years after his first public performance. The independent artist earned an eye-watering $21 million pretax in the 12 months prior to June 2017, thanks to more than 170 gigs with grosses north of $150,000 a show, Forbes estimates.

The white mask-wearing man's identity can be traced through music royalty manager BMI, where Marshmello is registered as a songwriter and performer. BMI's public database links pseudonyms with real names, so searching for Marshmello or for Comstock by his legal name rendered the same results, proving that Comstock is Marshmello.

This screenshot, captured on November 9th, shows Comstock listed as a songwriter on 11 Marshmello songs and one Dotcom and Borgore track, "Nope."BMI

This is just like searching for Aubrey Drake Graham and seeing Drake's discography, or looking up Marshall Mathers and finding the track listing for Eminem. From Lil Wayne to Lady Gaga, each artist's legal name renders the same song list as their stage name. Every pseudonym is identified by a different nine-digit number, or CAE/IPI; pseudonyms typically differ only slightly from the real name's CAE/IPI. With Marshmello and the other artists mentioned, there is a difference of 98 between the pseudonym CAE/IPI and legal name CAE/IPI. Such similarity is typically because applications for both the legal name and pseudonym were submitted in quick succession.

But just 12 hours after I sent an email for comment to Marshmello's management with links to BMI's database showing the same results for Comstock and Marshmello, Comstock had been wiped from BMI's website. Searching for Comstock as a songwriter rendered zero results. A Borgore and Dotcom track, "Nope," which had previously appeared in searches for both Comstock and his alias Marshmello, had also been removed from the online repertoire. Thankfully, previously-captured screenshots chronicle the credits. (As of this morning, Comstock had reappeared on the BMI website with a sole credit for "Nope.")

Through his manager, Marshmello, a 30 Under 30 Music Class of 2018 honoree, did not respond to a request for comment on his identity. When reached by phone, his lawyer declined to comment on his identity, as did his agent.

This screenshot, captured on November 9th, shows all of the songs Marshmello is listed as a songwriter on, including a Borgore and Dotcom track. They are the same songs that showed up in a search for Comstock prior to an email that alerted his management of the identical results.

Additional evidence comes from his holding company, Marshmello Creative, LLC. Formed in Delaware in August 2015, the only manager listed on the company is Christopher Comstock. Comstock is similarly listed as the sole manager of Dotcom Music, LLC, the organization attached to his previous moniker.

Marshmello's identity was also confirmed to Forbes by several industry insiders, who spoke on the condition of anonymity. Said one: "It's absolutely the same guy."

Marshmello's identity has been an open secret in the EDM community for some time. Social media is to blame for many of the clues: In 2016, Twitter users compiled photos of Comstock's leg tattoo and Marshmello in ripped jeans that apparently revealed the same ink. Others even stitched together photos of his hands and neck to point out similarities. Skrillex posted a video to Facebook on May 20th 2016 with the caption "happy bday [sic] Marshmello" that apparently pictured a crowd singing happy birthday to the masked producer. Comstock's birthday, according to his voter registration, is May 19th.

In March, another producer apparently accidentally caught Marshmello without his helmet in an Instagram story video. As recently as July, Bollywood actress Urvashi Rauteli posted a picture of Comstock in which she tagged Marshmello, though she edited the photo a few days later to remove the tag. (The edit history is still visible on Facebook.)

This screenshot, captured on November 9th, shows the edit history of a photo in which Rautela previously tagged an unmasked Marshmello.

As Forbes previously reported, further indication comes from credits by royalty monitor ASCAP, which have since been edited--just like BMI's database. In August, Marshmello's "Silence" listed Comstock as the only songwriter on the track aside from Khalid, who is the featured vocalist. Following the publication of my article, it was subsequently updated to credit Marshmello instead of Comstock.

Internet sleuths reportedly spotted a similar error on the credits of Far East Movement and Marshmello's 2016 "Freal Luv," though that has also since been amended. An ASCAP representative was unable to comment on the changes, noting only that the database is updated weekly.

This screenshot, captured in August, credits Comstock as a writer on Marshmello track "Silence." The credits were updated following the August publication of the image.Screenshot courtesy ASCAP

Marshmello has avoided publicly discussing his identity, joking instead on social media: "My name is Chris...short for Christmas came early"

My name is Chris...short for Christmas came early

— marshmello (@marshmellomusic) July 8, 2015

But pseudo-anonymity has served as a useful marketing tool for the artist, who had already tried to make it as Dotcom with a different sound and his name and face public. "We were like, 'How do we create something that's not driven by who it is, or what it's about?'" his manager Moe Shalizi told Forbes in August. "We're creating more of a faceless brand."

It's worked: "Alone" went platinum last month while its video has nearly 140 million views on YouTube. His touring prowess is such that he can book up to three gigs in one day. "The demand for Marshmello exceeds his availability," noted Steve Gordon, Marshmello's booking agent and co-owner at Circle Talent Agency.

For fans, whether he is Comstock or Marshmello is unlikely to make much difference. He has proven his ability to sell out shows and make hit songs--the recent "Wolves," featuring Selena Gomez, peaked at No.1 on Billboard's Hot Dance/Electronic Songs. Many are already aware of the link: Followers of Comstock's Dotcom Instagram regularly make comments related to "Marshmello" or "mello gang," while commentators on Marshmello's social media frequently reference Dotcom.

Perhaps Comstock could even be protecting his career if he takes off the mask. While Marshmello remains helmeted, he is potentially replaceable by other entities operating under the Marshmello brand.

In an video interview published in June, Marshmello--who doesn't speak to journalists--was asked if he would ever reveal himself. His response: Two thumbs down. He tweeted two months later: "I don't take my helmet off because I don't want or need fame. I'm genuinely trying to create something positive for people to connect with."

His fans will dance on regardless.
